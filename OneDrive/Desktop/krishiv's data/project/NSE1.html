NIFTY

A stock market index is created by grouping together similar kinds of stock. Sensex, which stands for 'Stock Exchange Sensitive Index', is the stock market index for the Bombay Stock Exchange. Nifty stands for 'National Stock Exchange Fifty' and is the index for the National Stock Exchange.Nifty is also called the CNX Nifty or the Nifty 50. It was launched in 1996 and managed by NSE Indices Limited. It is a popular barometer to check the performance of the stock market. For instance, if the stock market was up today, it usually means the Nifty 50 was up today.
The Nifty 50 index is a well-diversified 50 companies index reflecting overall market conditions. Nifty 50 Index is computed using free float market capitalization method. Nifty 50 can be used for a variety of purposes such as benchmarking fund portfolios, launching of index funds, ETFs and structured products.

Factors that cause changes in the Nifty:

1. Demand and supply

Demand and supply are the top factors that can drive stock prices up or down. This is because at the end of the day, the stock market is also just a market. Stocks with greater demand may witness a rise in the prices, while stocks with an inflated supply will witness falling prices. Over time, the proportion of demand and supply tends to fluctuate, leading to price movements in the market. There is no easy way to predict the demand and the supply for a stock, but you can view the typical demand and supply for stock during a given trading day on your trading portal, using the bid and ask figures.


2. Company metrics

The demand and the supply are, in turn, affected by various factors. These factors indirectly affect the prices of stocks by influencing the demand-supply equation. Top among these are the internal metrics of the company. Some of these factors can have a positive effect on the prices of the company’s stock. These aspects include good financial performance, dividend declaration by the company and the launch of a successful product or service. On the other hand, some company-related factors can drive down the price of the company’s shares. Examples of these metrics include scandals or scams involving the company, major layoffs or poor management performance.


3. Industry particulars and developments

Apart from developments within the company itself, the way the industry performs is also important in deciding the price movements of a company’s stock. If the industry performs well or receives any favourable news, the stock prices of all the companies belonging to that industry tend to benefit from these developments. For instance, subsidies announced by the government or a boost in the overall demand for the industry’s products or services have a positive impact on the companies in that industry. But falling demand, eroding customer base or other such negative developments have an adverse effect on the stock prices.


4. General market trends

Moving beyond a particular industry and taking a look at the market itself, there are broad trends that affect the prices of stocks of every listed company. Market phases or trends can be bullish or bearish. Bullish phases have a favourable impact on the prices of stocks in general, while bear markets witness falling stock prices. Typically, in a bullish market, investors are optimistic about the future and therefore drive up the demand. As you may have guessed, this, in turn, drives up the prices of stocks. But in bearish market phases, investors tend to offload their holdings, leading to falling stock prices in general.


5. Geopolitical factors

The stock market is not a separate entity. It is invariably linked to various national and international developments. And geopolitical factors, whether within India or across the globe, can significantly influence market prices either way. Some common examples of geopolitical developments or incidents that can impact the prices of shares include elections in a country, civil unrest, war and even natural disasters. All these developments tend to drive investor sentiment either upward or downward, leading to increased market activity. Needless to say, favourable geopolitical events lead to an increase in prices, while adverse events can pull the prices downward.
